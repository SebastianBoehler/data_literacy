Delay Analysis — Data Literacy Project

Overview

This project analyzes public transit delays in the Tübingen bus network using real-time data collected from the TRIAS API between November 2025 and January 2026. The visualization below shows the network topology with edges colored by average delay: gray indicates punctual service, while yellow to red indicates increasing delays.

Methodology

Data was collected continuously from the TRIAS real-time transit API, capturing planned and estimated arrival/departure times for all bus stops in the Tübingen network. Delays are calculated as the difference between estimated and planned times. The network graph is constructed by connecting consecutive stops within each journey, with edge weights representing the mean delay observed on that segment.

Key Findings

The analysis reveals that most bus segments operate with minimal delays (under 1 minute on average), indicated by gray coloring in the network map. However, certain routes and time periods show consistently higher delays, particularly during peak hours and on routes passing through the city center. The interactive visualization allows exploration of delay patterns by individual bus line.

Delay Analysis

The following plots show the delay distribution and patterns for the selected time period. Use the Time Period selector above to compare before and after the schedule change.

ECDF vs Histogram Comparison

Left: Traditional histogram showing delay counts. Right: Empirical CDF with 95% DKW confidence bands. ECDFs allow direct probability readings (e.g., P(delay ≤ 0) = 34.6%) without arbitrary bin choices.

Combined Overview

(A) Delay distribution histogram. (B) Hourly patterns with CI. (C) Empirical CDF with 95% DKW bands. (D) Top 15 delayed lines.

Late Rate vs Mean Delay Comparison

Left: Mean delay by hour (sensitive to outliers). Right: Late rate P(delay > 2 min) by hour. Late rate is more interpretable and robust for skewed data. Both show 95% bootstrap CI.

Late Rate by Hour (Bootstrap CI)

Probability of a bus being more than 2 minutes late, by hour of day. Shaded area shows 95% bootstrap confidence interval. More interpretable than mean delay for skewed data.

Weather Effect (Bootstrap CI)

Late rate by temperature range with 95% bootstrap confidence intervals. Sample sizes shown above each bar. Colder temperatures appear associated with fewer delays.

Weather Effect (Full Version)

Extended weather effect analysis with more temperature bins and sample size annotations.

Schedule Change Comparison (ECDF)

Empirical cumulative distribution functions comparing delay distributions before and after the Dec 14, 2025 schedule change. Shaded bands show 95% DKW confidence intervals. Orange curve (after) shifted left indicates improvement.

Schedule Change (Full Comparison)

Two-panel comparison: Left shows ECDF overlay, Right shows key metrics (on-time rate, late rate, median) with bootstrap confidence intervals. Late rate dropped from 31.5% to 22.6% after schedule change.

Quantile Analysis

Delay percentiles (50th, 75th, 90th, 95th, 99th) with 95% bootstrap CIs. More robust than means for skewed data. Key insight: 90% of buses arrive within 5.1 min of schedule. 90th percentile improved from 7.3 to 4.1 min after schedule change.

Top Stops by Late Rate

Spatial heterogeneity: which stops have the highest probability of delays? Tübingen Uni/Neue Aula has ~95% late rate. Sample sizes shown - some stops have wide CIs due to fewer observations.

Day of Week Effect

Left: Late rate by day of week (blue=weekday, orange=weekend). Tuesday/Thursday have highest delays. Right: Weekday vs weekend comparison shows weekdays are 1.9 percentage points more delayed than weekends.

Outlier Analysis

Documentation of extreme delays (>30 min). Only 0.85% of records are extreme outliers. Jan 25, 2026 had the most disruptions (253 records). These outliers inflate mean statistics but not medians.
